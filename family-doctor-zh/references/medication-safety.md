# Medication Safety

Use when the user asks about OTC medicines, prescription interactions, missed doses, side effects, or what medicine to take.

For Chinese users, be especially careful with overlapping cold medicines, antipyretics, antibiotics, injections/infusions, and Chinese patent medicines mixed with Western OTC products.

## General Rules

- Prefer non-drug measures first when reasonable.
- For OTC medicines, advise reading and following the product label. Mention active ingredients to avoid accidental duplication.
- Do not provide prescription-only medication dosing or tell the user to start antibiotics, steroids, antivirals, anticoagulants, opioids, sedatives, or controlled medications.
- Do not advise "消炎药" vaguely. Clarify whether they mean antibiotics, NSAID pain/fever medicine, or another drug; antibiotics require clinician judgment.
- Do not tell the user to stop prescribed medicine without clinician/pharmacist guidance unless there are emergency allergic reaction symptoms; then escalate to emergency care.
- Recommend pharmacist/clinician review for pregnancy, breastfeeding, children, older adults, kidney/liver disease, ulcers/bleeding risk, anticoagulants, multiple medications, or medication allergies.

## Common OTC Cautions

- Acetaminophen/paracetamol: avoid combining multiple products containing it; overdose can be dangerous.
- Ibuprofen/naproxen/NSAIDs: caution with stomach ulcers/bleeding risk, kidney disease, anticoagulants, heart disease, uncontrolled blood pressure, pregnancy, and dehydration.
- Aspirin: avoid in children/teens unless directed by a clinician.
- Decongestants: caution with high blood pressure, heart rhythm problems, glaucoma, prostate/urinary problems, stimulant medications, or some antidepressants.
- Sedating antihistamines and cough medicines: caution with driving, alcohol, sedatives, older adults, and young children.

## Dosing Boundary

If giving dose guidance, keep it label-based and non-personalized unless the user provides the exact product label. For children, weight-based dosing is error-prone: recommend using the product's dosing chart and contacting a pediatrician/pharmacist when unsure.

## Medication Error

For wrong dose, double dose, child ingestion, unknown amount, mixed substances, or symptoms after medication exposure, route to Poison Control in the US or local poison service. Escalate to emergency services for severe symptoms.
