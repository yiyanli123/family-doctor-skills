# Older Adults

Use for older adults, especially frailty, dementia, falls, multiple medications, chronic disease, or living alone. Older adults may have serious illness without classic symptoms; lower the threshold for same-day care.

## First Questions

Ask as needed:

- Age, baseline function, frailty, dementia/confusion baseline, living alone or caregiver available.
- Main symptom and onset; compare with usual baseline.
- Vital signs if available: temperature, blood pressure, pulse, oxygen saturation, glucose in diabetes.
- Chronic disease: heart/lung/kidney/liver disease, diabetes, stroke history, cancer, immune suppression.
- Current medicines, especially anticoagulants/antiplatelets, insulin/sulfonylureas, blood pressure medicines, sedatives, opioids, diuretics, steroids.
- Recent fall, head impact, infection exposure, dehydration, poor intake, urine changes.

## Emergency Now

Call 120/local emergency services for:

- Chest pain/pressure, severe shortness of breath, blue lips, fainting, severe weakness, or oxygen saturation clearly low for their usual range.
- Stroke signs: facial droop, arm/leg weakness, speech trouble, sudden confusion, sudden vision loss, severe sudden headache, trouble walking.
- New confusion, hard to wake, delirium, seizure, or sudden major behavior change.
- Fall with head impact while taking anticoagulants, loss of consciousness, repeated vomiting, severe headache, new weakness/numbness, hip pain/inability to stand, or suspected fracture.
- Severe dehydration, black/bloody stool, vomiting blood, severe abdominal pain, or signs of shock.
- Severe allergic reaction, major bleeding, severe burn, or poisoning/medication overdose.

## Same-Day Care

Recommend same-day medical evaluation for:

- Fever or suspected infection with weakness, confusion, fast breathing, low oxygen, low blood pressure, or worsening chronic disease.
- New urinary symptoms with fever/confusion/flank pain; do not assume all confusion is a urinary infection.
- Poor oral intake, dizziness, repeated falls, new swelling, worsening cough, wheeze, or medication side effects.
- Any significant fall even without obvious injury if the person is frail, on anticoagulants, or cannot walk normally.

## Home Care When Mild

Home care can be considered only if symptoms are mild, the person is close to baseline, breathing and hydration are stable, no fall/head injury red flags exist, and a caregiver can monitor.

Suggest:

- Encourage fluids if not restricted for heart/kidney disease.
- Review recent medication changes.
- Monitor temperature, breathing, urine, mental status, blood pressure/glucose when relevant.
- Set a short reassessment window, often same day or next morning.

## Common Pitfalls

- Do not dismiss confusion, falls, weakness, or "just not right" as aging.
- Do not advise self-driving with chest pain, fainting, severe dizziness, stroke signs, or severe shortness of breath.
- Be cautious with sedating cold medicines, NSAIDs, dehydration, and duplicate medications.
- With anticoagulants, even seemingly minor head injury may need urgent evaluation.

## Family Output Pattern

```
老人和年轻人不一样，严重感染、心脑血管问题有时不一定表现得很典型。现在最需要看：是否比平时更糊涂/更虚弱、呼吸是否费力、有没有胸痛/偏瘫说话不清、有没有跌倒或头部撞击。

如果有以上任何一项，建议不要在家硬扛，直接急诊/120。
```
